# Camera Shots


## The Big Picture Shots

These shots are primarily used to set the scene and establish relationships between characters and their environments.

* Establishing Shot

    Description: Typically a very wide shot that shows the geography, time of day, and scale of subjects.

    When to use: To open a scene, transition between locations, or introduce a new world (crucial in genres like Sci-Fi). 

* Master Shot (The Master)

    Description: Captures the entire scene playing out, confirming location and clarifying where characters are in relation to one another.

    When to use: To show the group dynamic or relationships (e.g., a family dinner scene in The Godfather: Part II) and provide a safety net for editing.

* Wide Shot (WS)

    Description: Positions subjects far from the camera. Distinct from the establishing shot, this focuses more on the subject's scale relative to their surroundings.

    When to use: To make subjects appear lost, lonely, or overwhelmed by their environment.

## Body-Centric Shots

These shots focus on the physicality of the characters.

* Full Shot (FS)

    Description: Frames the subject’s entire body from head to toe.

    When to use: To make statements about a character's physicality, posture, or wardrobe, presenting them "in all their glory." 

* Medium Full Shot (Cowboy Shot)

    Description: Frames the subject from the top of the head to just below the waist. Originally popularized by Westerns to show gun holsters.

    When to use: To present a subject as confident, dangerous, or confrontational.

## Character & Emotion Shots

These shots are used to build intimacy and focus on dialogue or emotion.

* Medium Shot (MS)

    Description: The most popular and neutral shot in cinema. Frames from above the waist to just above the head.

    When to use: To capture a subject in a way that mimics reliable human interaction—neither too distant nor too dramatic. 

* Medium Close-Up (MCU)

    Description: Frames from mid-chest to just above the head.

    When to use: To reduce background distractions and prioritize story and character details without losing their physicality completely. 

* Close-Up (CU)

    Description: Frames the head and face, typically at eye level.
    When to use: The most powerful tool for highlighting emotional changes or dramatic beats. It acts as a "window to the soul."

## Detail Shots

* Extreme Close-Up (ECU)

    Description: Isolates a specific area, such as eyes, lips, or a specific object.
    When to use: To create intense emphasis or startling intimacy. 

* Insert Shot

    Description: A specific type of close-up that focuses on a prop or detail crucial to the narrative.


# Camera Framing


## Singles

A single shot features one character alone in the frame. It is the primary way to focus on an individual's performance.

* Clean Single

    Description: No part of any other character is visible. Best used to convey isolation.

* Dirty Single

    Description: Includes a limited presence (e.g., a shoulder or edge of a head) of another character. This maintains a physical connection between characters even when focusing on one.

## Two Shot

When two characters are visible together in the frame.

* Purpose: To create a visual relationship between two characters, whether affectionate, contentious, or otherwise.

* Requirement: Both faces must be clearly visible for it to be considered a true two shot.

## Group Shots

As more characters are added, the terminology adjusts (e.g., Three Shot).

* Crowd Shot

    Description: When numbering becomes impractical (4, 5, etc.), it is simply referred to as a crowd shot.

    Regardless of the number, the goal is always to establish the relationships between the characters in the frame.

* Over-the-Shoulder (OTS)

    Description: The camera looks past one character's shoulder to focus on the other.

    Purpose: The most common way to cover conversation. It provides perspective from both sides and makes the audience feel included in the interaction.

* Point of View (POV)

    Description: A shot that mimics the visual perspective of a character.

    Versatility: Can represent the view of a person, alien, camera, spirit, or even an animal (like a shark).

    Audio Pairing: Often paired with "POV audio" so the audience hears exactly what the character hears.

* Insert Shot

    Description: A shot that focuses on a specific detail, often an object.

    Framing Importance: It's not just about being tight on a detail; how it is framed matters.

    Combinations: Can be combined with POV shots to show a character looking at a specific item (e.g., a "first-person" insert vs. a "third-person" insert).


# Camera Angles


## Power Dynamic Angles

These angles are primarily used to establish power, weakness, or vulnerability.

* Low Angle Shot

    Description: The camera is positioned below the subject's eyeline, looking up.

    Effect: Makes the subject appear powerful, dominant, or threatening. It works for both heroes and villains (e.g., Scar in The Lion King).

* High Angle Shot

    Description: The camera looks down on the subject from above.

    Effect: Diminishes the character, making them appear weak, vulnerable, or powerless. Often paired with low angle shots to emphasize an imbalance of power.

* Overhead Shot (Bird's Eye / God's Eye View)

    Description: The camera shoots straight down from directly above the subject (approx. 90 degrees).

    Effect: Showcases complex movements, suggests a divine connection, or captures horrific details (e.g., a crime scene). It can also show disconnection, as seen in The Master.

* Dutch Angle

    Description: The camera is tilted so the horizon line is not level.

    Effect: Creates a sense of unease, mania, tension, or disorientation. Spike Lee uses this in Do the Right Thing to intensify racial tension.

## Neutral & Connection Angles

These angles are grounded in natural human perception.

* Eye Level Shot

    Description: The most common camera height, positioned at the subject's eye level.

    Effect: Neutral and non-judgmental. It mimics how we naturally regard people. When combined with breaking the fourth wall (e.g., The Wolf of Wall Street), it creates an instant rapport.

* Shoulder Level Shot

    Description: The camera is positioned just below eye level, at the shoulder.

    Effect: A standard runner-up to eye level, often used in conversations (frequently turning into Over-The-Shoulder shots). It can subtly emphasize height and power differences.

## Action & Detail Angles

These angles focus on specific body parts or movement through an environment.

* Hip Level Shot

    Description: The camera is positioned at the subject's hips.

    Effect: A staple of the Western genre because it aligns with gun holsters (e.g., The Good, the Bad and the Ugly).

* Knee Level Shot

    Description: The camera is placed at knee height.

    Effect: Can double as a low angle but is specifically useful for tracking movement through an environment or showcasing details like the leg braces in Forrest Gump.

* Ground Level Shot

    Description: The camera is placed on or slightly below the ground.

    Effect: Provides a stylish way to track movement or capture setting details. A famous example is the tricycle tracking shot in The Shining.


# Depth of Field


## Focus as a State
These techniques describe the static state of focus within a shot.

* Deep Focus

    Description: Both the subject (foreground) and the background are in crisp detail.

    When to use: Great for group shots, showcasing important background details, or creating layered images where action plays out on multiple planes simultaneously (e.g., the opening sequence of Gra vity).

* Shallow Focus

    Description: A specific portion of the frame (the "plane of focus") is sharp, while the rest is soft and blurry. This often creates the "bokeh" effect.

    When to use: To isolate a subject, suggest an emotional state, or guide the audience's attention to something specific (e.g., Moonlight, where extreme shallow focus emphasizes the mother's manipulative influence).

* Soft Focus

    Description: No portion of the frame is 100% sharp. Achieved through lens imperfections, diffusion filters, or even stockings/Vaseline on the lens.

    When to use: Historically used to beautify stars, but now used to create dream sequences, flashbacks, or a surreal atmosphere (e.g., A.I. Artificial Intelligence).

## Focus as an Action
These techniques involve changing focus or using specialized lenses to alter perception.

* Rack Focus

    Description: The act of transitioning shallow focus from one subject to another within the same shot. It is a verb ("racking focus"), not just a state.

    When to use: To direct attention with pinpoint precision, reveal new elements, or show a connection between two subjects without cutting (e.g., The Host).

* Split Diopter

    Description: Uses a specialized lens to keep two separate focal points (usually foreground and deep background) sharp while the area in between creates a blur.

    When to use: When you need deep focus on two specific subjects but lack the lighting or distance for natural deep focus. It creates an unnatural, "impossible" perspective often used by Quentin Tarantino (e.g., The Hateful Eight).

* Tilt Shift

    Description: A lens that bends light to create distortions where only a narrow slice of the frame is sharp.

    When to use: To make life-size locations look like miniature models (e.g., The Social Network) or to visualize an altered mental state (e.g., A Serious Man).


# Camera Gear


## Handheld & Support Rigs

* Handheld Shot

    Description: Taken with a shoulder rig or EasyRig, often resulting in shaky movement.

    Effect: Adds intensity, chaos, or documentary-style realism (e.g., Hawkeye's family scene in Avengers: Endgame). It can also create intimacy by making the viewer feel present.

* Tripod (Sticks)

    Description: A fixed three-legged support. A "fluid head" allows for smooth pans and tilts.

    Effect: Ideal for static shots where you don't want to distract from the subject, such as dialogue scenes or precise comedy (e.g., The Grand Budapest Hotel).

* Pedestal

    Description: A mechanism that moves the camera vertically up and down.

    Effect: Used to match an actor's movement or build suspense by revealing hidden details (e.g., the "bomb under the table" reveal in Inglourious Basterds).

## Movement Rigs

* Crane / Jib

    Description: An arm that extends the camera outward to glide smoothly in any direction.

    Effect: Captures large movements to highlight emotions like isolation, freedom, or spectacle (e.g., the long take in Spectre).

* Overhead Rig

    Description: Positions the camera directly above the subject for a top-down view.

    Effect: Creates an objective, "God's eye" perspective. It can suggest omniscience (e.g., the casino boss in Casino) or detail in inserts.

* Dolly

    Description: A wheeled apparatus on tracks (straight or curved).

    Effect: Smoothly pushes in/out or tracks lateral movement without the distraction of shake. Famous for long takes like in Oldboy.

* Slider / Cable Cam

    Description: Practical alternatives to dollies. Sliders are smaller for similar effects; cable cams cover long distances or glide past obstructions.

* Stabilizer (Steadicam / Gimbal)

    Description: Compensates for camera shake using counterweights or motors, allowing the operator to move freely.

    Effect: Eliminates shake for organic, fluid movement through complex sets (e.g., the basement reveal in Parasite).

## Specialized & Action Rigs

* Snorricam (Body Cam)

    Description: Rigs the camera directly to the actor's body, facing them.

    Effect: Locks the character in the frame while the background moves, creating vertigo, panic, or a subjective sense of tragedy (e.g., Requiem for a Dream).

* Vehicle Mount

    Description: Mounts the camera to a car, bike, or plane.

    Effect: Captures realism and intensity in action sequences (e.g., Tom Cruise hanging off a plane in Mission: Impossible - Rogue Nation).

* Drone

    Description: A remote-controlled aircraft for aerial shots.

    Effect: Makes grand establishing shots or chase scenes accessible. Can combine aerial views with close-ups in a single shot (e.g., Bohemian Rhapsody).

* Motion Control

    Description: A robotic arm that allows for precise repetition of camera movement.

    Effect: Essential for visual effects where multiple takes need to be composited together seamlessly (e.g., the Winklevoss twins in The Social Network).

* Underwater Housing

    Description: A waterproof case for the camera.

    Effect: Allows filming submerged shots, creating an immersive or anxious feeling (e.g., the swimming lesson in Moonlight).


# Camera Movement


## Static & Axis Movements

* Static Shot

    Description: The camera is locked on a tripod with zero movement.

    When to use: Ideal for dialogue, precise compositions, or allowing an actor's performance to shine. It can also trap a character to emphasize helplessness (e.g., 12 Years a Slave).

* Pan

    Description: The camera rotates horizontally (left or right) from a fixed position.

    When to use: To follow a character or reveal information (a Wes Anderson staple).

* Whip Pan

    Description: A rapid pan used to heighten energy or show a relationship between characters (e.g., Whiplash).

* Tilt

    Description: The camera directs upward or downward from a fixed position.

    When to use: To capture verticality, dominance, vulnerability, or scale (e.g., tilting up to show the bending city in Inception).

* Roll

    Description: The camera rotates on its long axis (like a barrel roll).

    When to use: To create disorientation or unsettle the equilibrium. It can reinforce a thematic shift in power (e.g., The Joker's upside-down reveal in The Dark Knight).

## Depth Movements

* Push In

    Description: The camera physically moves towards the subject.

    When to use: To emphasize a moment, signal importance, or visualize internal conflict (e.g., Michael Corleone’s decision in The Godfather).

* Pull Out

    Description: The camera physically moves away from the subject.

    When to use: To de-emphasize the subject, unveil context/setting, or highlight isolation and abandonment (e.g., the final shot of Catch Me If You Can).

* Zoom

    Description: Changing the focal length of the lens to appear closer or further away (no physical camera movement).

    When to use: Creates an unnatural movement (since human eyes can't zoom). Slow zooms build uneasiness (e.g., The Shining), while "Crash Zooms" add dramatic or comedic effect (e.g., Django Unchained).

* Dolly Zoom (The Vertigo Effect)

    Description: Moving the camera physically (dolly) while zooming in the opposite direction.

    When to use: Creates a warping effect that represents internal or external conflict (e.g., Jaws, Raging Bull) or intimacy (e.g., Bohemian Rhapsody).

## Tracking & Dynamic Movements

* Tracking Shot

    Description: The camera physically moves with a subject through a scene.

    When to use: To follow a character's journey ("Where are they going?"), build tension (e.g., Elephant), or create a documentary-style immersion (e.g., Three Billboards Outside Ebbing, Missouri).

* Trucking Shot

    Description: The camera moves laterally (left or right) through the scene.

    When to use: To follow a character sideways or establish a "storybook" tableau (e.g., the camp inspection in Moonrise Kingdom).

* Arc Shot

    Description: The camera orbits around a subject.

    When to use: Adds dynamism to static characters. It can heroically unify a team (e.g., The Avengers) or underscore tension and panic (e.g., The Dark Knight).

* Boom Shot

    Description: The camera moves vertically up or down through space (using a crane/jib).

    When to use: To reveal information or follow a character's vertical action (e.g., Full Metal Jacket).

* Random Movement

    Description: Movement without control—shakes, incidental zooms, and handheld jitters.

    When to use: To create a documentary look, imply real-time events, or reflect a character's chaotic state of mind (e.g., The Big Short, Se7en).


# Camera Lenses


## Lens Basics

Focal Length: The distance (in mm) between the optical center of the lens and the image sensor.

Short Focal Length (e.g., 24mm): Wider angle of view.

Long Focal Length (e.g., 100mm): Narrower angle of view.

Prime vs. Zoom:

Prime: Fixed focal length. Generally produces sharper images and has larger apertures (better for low light and shallow depth of field).

Zoom: Variable focal length. Offers versatility (wide, medium, close-up) without changing lenses, saving time on set.

## The Wide End

* Extreme Wide-Angle Lens (18mm - 24mm)

    Description: Captures a massive field of view, often distorting the image near the edges.

    Effect: Can capture close-ups and vast landscapes simultaneously (e.g., The Revenant). When distorted (Fisheye), it underscores absurdity (e.g., The Favourite) or a warped mental state (e.g., Fear and Loathing in Las Vegas).

* Wide-Angle Lens (24mm - 35mm)

    Description: Provides a wide field of view with less distortion than extreme wides.

    Effect: Accentuates movement (e.g., trucking shots in Punch-Drunk Love) and can exaggerate distance between characters to make them feel far apart (e.g., Drive).

## The Middle Ground

* Standard Lens (35mm - 50mm)

    Description: Also called "Normal Lenses." The focal length mimics the human eye’s field of view.

    Effect: Creates a natural, grounded, and unbiased presentation. It makes the audience feel present in the story without "technology" getting in the way (e.g., Call Me by Your Name, The Godfather).

## The Long End

* Telephoto Lens (70mm+)

    Description: Long lenses that have a narrow field of view.

    Effect:

Voyeurism: Makes the viewer feel like they are observing from afar (e.g., Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy, Captain Phillips).

Compression: Flattens space, making background and foreground appear closer together. This can isolate a character in a crowd (e.g., The Pursuit of Happyness) or make running appear futile (e.g., The Graduate).

## Specialty Lenses

* Macro Lens

    Description: Defined by the ability to focus extremely close to a subject, not a specific focal length.

Effect: Acts like a magnifying glass for details, perfect for extreme close-ups of clues or insects (e.g., David Fincher’s Se7en).

* Tilt Shift Lens

    Description: The lens mechanism can tilt or shift to manipulate the plane of focus.

    Effect: Can make real-world locations look like miniature models (e.g., Game Night) or create a dreamy, vintage look with specific distortions (e.g., the "Deakinizer" lens in The Assassination of Jesse James).

# Frame Rate   

## The Basics

Frame Rate (FPS): The number of individual frames shown within one second.

The Illusion of Motion: Movies are actually a series of still images shown fast enough that the brain blends them together (Persistence of Vision).

Standard Cinematic Frame Rate (24 fps): The industry standard for a "cinematic" look. It was originally chosen as the minimum speed to support sound while saving film stock, but it has become the aesthetic standard for storytelling.

High Frame Rate (HFR): Rates higher than 24 fps (e.g., 48 fps for The Hobbit, 120 fps for Gemini Man).

Effect: Reduces motion blur and makes action appear hyper-realistic or "smoother," though it can look like a soap opera or video game to some audiences.

## Manipulating Time

* Fast Motion (Undercranking)

    How it works: You shoot at a lower frame rate (e.g., 6 fps) and play it back at the standard 24 fps. Because fewer frames were captured, the action appears to speed up.

    When to use: To create a comedic effect (e.g., Austin Powers), a vintage home-movie look, or a chaotic, jittery aesthetic.

* Step Printing

    How it works: Shooting at a low frame rate with a slow shutter speed, then printing (duplicating) frames to reach 24 fps.

    Effect: Creates a blurry, streaky, and disjointed look, often used to show a drug-induced state or disorientation (e.g., Chungking Express, Gladiator).

* Slow Motion (Overcranking)

    How it works: You shoot at a higher frame rate (e.g., 48, 60, 120+ fps) and play it back at standard 24 fps. Because more frames were captured, the action stretches out and slows down.

    When to use: To emphasize action or emotion, make horror more terrifying, or capture details the human eye can't see (e.g., explosions in The Hurt Locker, bullets in The Matrix).

* Speed Ramping

    How it works: An editing technique that transitions between varying speeds (slow motion to normal/fast) within the same shot.

    Effect: Gives specific impacts extra weight, such as the fight scenes in 300.

* Capture vs. Presentation Rate

    Capture Rate: How many frames per second the camera records.

    Presentation Rate: How many frames per second the audience sees (usually 24 fps for film).

### The Golden Rule

For Normal Motion: Capture Rate must equal Presentation Rate (e.g., Shoot 24 fps, Project 24 fps).

For Slow Motion: Capture Rate > Presentation Rate (e.g., Shoot 60 fps, Project 24 fps).

For Fast Motion: Capture Rate < Presentation Rate (e.g., Shoot 12 fps, Project 24 fps).


# Scenes Transitions


## The Fundamental Transitions

* The Cut

    Description: An instant switch from one shot to another.

    Function: The most basic and common way to transition. It is the invisible glue of filmmaking.

* The Fade

    Description: A shot dissolves to or from a solid color (usually black or white).

    Function:

    Fade to Black: Often used to open a film (easing the audience in) or close a "chapter" (providing a moment to breathe), as seen in Pulp Fiction.

Fade to White: Represents entering a dream, death, or ambiguity (e.g., the ending of Black Swan).

* The Dissolve

    Description: A gradual transition where one shot fades directly into the next.

    Function: Suggests a longer passage of time, a memory, or a dream.

    Superimposition: When the dissolve is held, showing both shots simultaneously to create a thematic comparison (e.g., Michael Corleone and his father in The Godfather Part II).

## Connecting Time & Space

* Match Cut

    Description: A transition that uses similar visual elements (shapes, composition, movement) or audio to connect two different scenes instantly.

    Function: Bridges vast jumps in time or space while drawing a subconscious connection between the two subjects (e.g., the bone-to-spaceship cut in 2001: A Space Odyssey).

* Match Dissolve

    Description: Combines a match cut with a dissolve.

    Function: Smooths over the abruptness of a cut while maintaining the thematic connection (e.g., Jessie’s face dissolving into the sun in Toy Story 2).

## Stylized & Dynamic Transitions

* Iris

    Description: An old-fashioned effect where a black circle opens or closes to reveal/hide the image.

    Function: Historically used like a fade. Modern usage focuses attention on a specific detail or signifies a stylistic homage (e.g., the police headquarters reveal in The Departed).

* Wipe

    Description: One shot is replaced by another moving into the frame from a specific direction (up, down, left, right) or shape (star, clock).

    Function: Famous in Star Wars (inspired by Kurosawa and Flash Gordon). Often used today for a vintage, comic-book, or campy aesthetic.

* Passing Transition (Pass-By)

    Description: A character or object moves across the frame (or the camera moves past an object), completely blocking the view to hide the cut.

    Function: Seamless and energetic. Edgar Wright uses this frequently to maintain momentum without skipping a beat.

* Whip Pan

    Description: A rapid camera rotation that blurs the image to transition to the next shot.

    Function: Sustains high energy and momentum, often reflecting a hectic or dizzying state (e.g., the drug-fueled montage in Boogie Nights).

## Impact & Audio Transitions

* Smash Cut

    Description: An abrupt cut between two contrasting scenes (e.g., quiet to loud, chaotic to still).

    Function: Used to shock or startle the audience, such as waking from a nightmare (Shutter Island) or for comedic punctuation.

* J-Cut & L-Cut

    J-Cut (Pre-lap): Audio from the next scene starts before the video cuts. Great for leading into a flashback or dialogue.

    L-Cut: Audio from the previous scene continues into the next shot. Often used for narration or to let a reaction linger.


# Editing Techniques


* Eyeline Match

    Description: An editor cuts to the object of a character's attention immediately after the character looks at it.

    Function: It is a basic visual language that connects the dots between what a character sees, thinks, or feels, and it naturally motivates the cut.

    Common Forms:

    * Shot-Reverse-Shot: Cutting back and forth between two subjects in conversation using a reverse angle. This is crucial for maintaining scene geography and clarity during dialogue.

    * Insert: A close-up shot of a detail (e.g., a prop or a clock) prompted by the character's gaze to emphasize a specific element.

* Cross-Cutting

    Description: Cutting back and forth between two or more scenes happening in different locations, usually at the same time. This creates a simultaneous progression of events.

    Function:

    Juxtaposition: Creates an ironic or dramatic comparison between events (e.g., the baptism and assassination sequence in The Godfather).

Building Tension: Keeps the plot and action moving forward by alternating between various battlefronts or subplots (e.g., the final act of Rogue One).

* Eye Trace (Center Punching)

    Description: The editor cuts between two or more shots that focus the audience's attention on the same area of the frame (often the center).

    Function: Prevents the viewer's eye from having to scan the frame after each cut. This technique is heavily used in fast-paced action scenes to allow the viewer to process even chaotic movement (e.g., Mad Max: Fury Road).

* Split Edits (J-Cut and L-Cut)

    Description: Techniques where audio and video are separated to create a smoother or more nuanced transition.

    J-Cut (Pre-lap): The audio of the next scene begins before the visual cut occurs. The edit looks like the letter "J" on a timeline.

    L-Cut: The audio of the preceding scene carries over into the visual of the next scene. The edit looks like the letter "L" on a timeline.

    Function: Creates more natural dialogue rhythm, allows the capture of reaction shots, and helps visually explain a plan or sequence (e.g., the break-in plan in Mission Impossible: Rogue Nation).

* Intellectual Montage

    Description: The juxtaposition of seemingly unrelated images to create a new feeling or conceptual idea. It is based on the Kuleshov Effect, where an audience derives meaning by intercutting a neutral face with an emotionally charged image.

    Function: Evokes emotion, visually reiterates a larger theme, or forces the audience to make connections themselves (e.g., the sequence illustrating the global scale of disaster in Don't Look Up).

* Cut on Action

    Description: Cutting during a character or object’s movement (e.g., cutting from a wide shot of a punch being thrown to a close-up of the impact). The out-point of the first shot corresponds directly to the in-point of the next.

    Function: Creates a seamless transition that feels natural to the audience. It is often used in fight scenes to keep up the pace and give extra force or weight to the action.

## The Six Criteria for a Cut

The most successful cut maximizes emotional impact while preserving narrative clarity and spatial continuity. The percentage figures Murch gave represent the relative value he assigns to each point:

1. Emotion (51%): The cut must be true to the emotion of the moment or the scene. This is the most crucial element; if the audience doesn't feel the right emotion, the cut fails.

    Example: If a character is panicking, quick, frantic cuts (fast pace) enhance the emotion, regardless of whether they perfectly follow spatial rules.

2. Story (23%): The cut must advance the story. Every edit should move the plot forward or add essential information to the narrative.

3. Rhythm (10%): The cut must occur at a rhythmically interesting and "right" moment. This relates to the scene's tempo and pace—the editor should feel the rhythm of the action and the actors' performances.

4. Eye-Trace (7%): The cut should consider where the audience's eyes are focused on the screen before the cut, and ensure that focus is carried naturally to the point of interest in the next shot. This prevents the viewer from having to scan the frame and keeps the viewing experience seamless.

5. Two-Dimensional Plane of Screen (5%): The cut must maintain the established two-dimensional continuity (i.e., screen direction). This is related to the 180-degree rule and avoiding cuts that make objects jump horizontally across the screen.

6. Three-Dimensional Space of Action (4%): The cut must maintain the established three-dimensional continuity of the physical space. This is concerned with geography—ensuring the audience knows where objects and characters are in relation to each other.


# Composition


1. Points

* Description: Focal points (small darts, intersections, or subjects) used to draw the viewer's attention.

* Techniques: Often used in conjunction with established guidelines like the Rule of Thirds (placing the subject at one of the four grid intersections) or the Golden Triangle (using diagonal lines to create two balanced focal points).

2. Lines

* Description: The fundamental building blocks that create direction and mood.

* Types:

    * Vertical Lines: Suggest height and strength.

    * Horizontal Lines: Suggest distance and calm.

    * Diagonals: Add off-kilter energy.

    * Leading Lines: Used to physically pull the eye toward the main subject of the frame (e.g., the hallway lines in a Christopher Nolan film).

3. Shapes

* Description: Geometric (triangles, squares) or organic forms that bring visual unity.

* Function:

    * Geometric: Adds a tidy, satisfying feeling, suggesting order.

    * Frames within Frames: Using shapes (like windows or doorways) to create a secondary frame, focusing the viewer on a smaller area and evoking feelings like claustrophobia or comfort.

4. Textures and Patterns

* Description: The surface qualities (smooth, rough, patterned) of objects and the repetition of elements.

* Function:

    * Texture: Achieved through lighting and production design to make a subject pop or blend in, lending a more tangible connection to the imagery.

    * Patterns: Suggest ideas like power, control, or harmony. Breaking a pattern can be used to suggest something is out of place or wrong (e.g., the disorienting carpet in The Shining).

5. Space (Positive and Negative)

* Description: The division of the frame into the subject (positive space) and the empty area around it (negative space).

* Concepts:

    * Headroom: Negative space above the subject's head.

    * Lead Room (Looking Room): Negative space in front of a subject that suggests where they are going or looking.

    * Filling the Frame: Giving the subject most or all positive space to create sensory overload or chaos.

6. Depth

* Description: The illusion that turns a 2D image into a world by dividing the frame into foreground, middle ground, and background.

* Techniques:

    * Shallow Focus: Isolates the subject from the other planes using a small depth of field.

    * Deep Focus: Allows multiple layers to be in sharp focus simultaneously, creating a visual hierarchy of importance (e.g., Citizen Kane).

7. Balance

* Description: The distribution of visual weight in the frame to make it feel stable and organized.

* Techniques:

    * Rule of Odds: When shooting multiple subjects, an odd number (three, five) is often more visually pleasing.

    * Golden Ratio/Spiral: A geometric principle (related to the Fibonacci sequence) used to create natural balance in a composition.

    * Symmetry: Used by directors like Wes Anderson to create a purposefully organized or controlled feeling.

8. Angle

* Description: The camera's perspective on the subject, which affects the perceived power and mood.

* Types:

    * High Angle: Makes the subject seem small and less powerful.

    * Low Angle: Makes the subject seem large and more powerful.

    * Dutch Angle (Canted/Tilted): Tilting the camera to make the audience feel on edge or uneasy.

    * Overhead Angle: Gives a detached, objective perspective.

9. (Bonus) Color and Tone

* Description: The use of color schemes (monochromatic, complementary, triadic) and the brightness and contrast (tone) within an image.

* Function: Color can overrule other compositional elements by drawing the eye to the brightest or most contrasting element. High tonal contrast (dark vs. light) is often used to visualize themes like good versus evil (e.g., Black Swan).


# Lighting


This video, "Ultimate Guide to Cinematic Lighting — Types of Light & Gear Explained" by StudioBinder, is the twelfth episode in "The Shot List" series. It serves as Part 1 of a guide on cinematic lighting, covering the essential fundamentals, terminology, and gear used to shape light in filmmaking.

1. Lighting Fundamentals
Lighting is critical for visual storytelling, setting the mood, providing insight into a character, and guiding the viewer's eye. Decisions are made by the Director of Photography (DP) and executed by the Gaffer (head lighting electrician).

Types of Light

|  Category	| Description	| Examples
| --- | --- | ---
|Natural Light	|Any light that occurs naturally.	|Sunlight, Moonlight, Firelight.
|Artificial Light	|Any light that is man-made and powered by electricity.	|Studio lights, lamps.
|Ambient Lighting	|Lights that are naturally on location and not brought by the crew.	|Street lamps, neon signs, the Moon.
|Practical Lighting	|Any light source that can be seen within the frame.	|Lamps, candles, television screens.
|Motivated Lighting	|Light that has a logical justification in the scene (often from a practical light).	|A character standing by a window is lit by "daylight."

Properties of Light

* Luminance (Lumens): Measurement of how much light a source produces.

* Illuminance (Lux/Foot-Candles): Measurement of how much of that light hits the subject (incidental light).

* Reflective Light: The amount of light that reaches the camera.

* Light Falloff: The loss of light intensity as it moves away from the source. This is predicted by the Inverse Square Law (intensity is reduced by 75% every time you double the distance from the source). [03:46]

* Color Temperature (Kelvin): Measures the color hue of the light.

* Daylight: Cooler (bluer) at about 5600K.

* Tungsten: Warmer (more orange) at about 3200K.

2. Key Terminology

| Term	| Definition and Impact
| --- | ---
| Exposure	|The amount of light that reaches the camera sensor (controlled by aperture, shutter speed, and ISO).
| Contrast Ratio	|Measures the difference between light and shadow in an image.
| High-Key Lighting	|Uses a low contrast ratio where the scene is lit evenly with minimal shadows. Often used for brighter, happier scenes.
| Low-Key Lighting	|Uses a high contrast ratio with a large difference between light and shadow. Often used for darker, dramatic scenes.
| Chiaroscuro	|A type of low-key lighting with extreme contrast, often seen in film noir to create a mysterious and dangerous mood.
| Hard Light	|Directional light that creates stark, defined shadows. Used for intense or dramatic scenes.
| Soft Light	|Diffused light that generates less defined shadows. Used for romantic or happier scenes.
| Magic Hour (Golden Hour)	|Occurs just after sunrise and before sunset, providing naturally soft, less harsh, and colorful light.
| Broadside (Dumb Side)	|Lighting the side of a subject closer to the camera, making them appear more approachable.
| Short Side (Smart Side)	|Lighting the side of a subject not facing the camera, giving a more dramatic look.
| Lens Flare	|Light hitting the camera lens to create patterns and shapes, often for a stylized, aesthetic effect.
| Bokeh	|The out-of-focus elements in a shot (usually points of light), often adding a romantic or painterly texture.
3. Essential Lighting Gear
Light Sources [09:11]
Tungsten Lights: Powerful, warm color temperature (~3200K).

HMI Lights: Stronger than tungsten, cooler temperature (to imitate daylight, ~5600K).

Fluorescent/Kino Flos: Offer soft, cool light and are highly adjustable.

LED Lights: Provide a high amount of control over color and strength.

Light Measurement and Control
Gels: Thin plastic sheets used to adjust a light's color, hue, or intensity.

Light Meter: Measures the amount of incidental light hitting a subject to inform camera exposure settings.

Neutral Density (ND) Gels: Used to reduce the intensity of light without changing its color, often placed over lights or windows.

Flags: Opaque boards used to shape and block unwanted light.

Barn Doors: Adjustable flaps on the light itself that help direct the beam.

C-Stands: Versatile stands used for mounting lights and rigging flags, diffusers, and reflectors.

Diffusion and Redirection
Diffusion: Placing a semi-translucent material between the light source and the subject to scatter the light and make it softer.

Methods: Soft boxes, diffusion paper, silks, clouds/fog (natural).

Reflectors: Used to redirect and diffuse light by bouncing it back onto the subject. They come in various colors and textures.

Examples: B-boards (styrofoam), foam core.
